Willoughby's Beach - King Gizzard & the Lizard Wizard (2011)

Willoughby's Beach (2011) is King Gizzard and the Lizard Wizard's first official EP release and, although that comes through pretty clearly in the music, the record is still very enjoyable! Clocking in at barely under 23 minutes, Gizz gives us nine solid surf punk tracks that sound straight out of an Australian garage.

Easily the weakest aspect would have to be the lyrical content. Almost every song sees either one or two lines being repeated for the duration of the track. While the melodies to accompany these lines are perfectly lovely, the lyrics themselves can only do so much on their own and often leave me wanting for more especially with the hindsight of the evolutions that the writing style would go through as time goes on. For a basic surf punk album, these slackery lyrics and their delivery are all well and good, but there is definitely room to expand.

Speaking of the delivery, Stu and Cook absolutely kill the vocals on this EP. One of the most iconic things about KGLW aside from the sheer diversity of their musical repertoire would have to be the vocal style that simply fits their music to a T. There's just enough energy for the vocals to sound driven but not too much that it sounds forced or unrealistic: The EP really does sound like something that someone from your neighborhood could have made and I think that raw production really helps sell the vibes and aesthetics that KGLW was trying to go for. For what the lyrics themselves lack, Stu and Cook make up for almost all of it with the sheer charisma and punky delivery that they bring to the table.

The lead single Black Tooth, also the most popular track on Spotify, is an odd track to me. Although it's a pretty standout song when you listen to it individually, I catch myself completely missing it when I put the record on in full without looking at the track list. That could totally be a problem with me, though, haha.

This could be related to a classic styling of KGLW that they would go on to experiment with much more in future records: Tracks transitioning into each other. Although only three of the songs on Willoughby's do this (Lunch Meat ==> Crookedile ==> Let it Bleed), many of the other tracks feel like they sort of meld together. This has its strengths and its weaknesses--on one hand, it can make the experience of listening to the record feel a lot more cohesive and paint a vibe really well, but on the other, it can make songs have a lot more trouble standing out.

Out of all of the tracks, I could probably only reliably sing Danger $$$ if you asked me to. I might be able to remember the melody of Let it Bleed but you would have to remind me that it's the one that goes “I want to see my love bird” over and over again. The songs are good, don't get me wrong, but the blending of the tracks does not lend itself well to the admittedly poor identity that many of the songs already have.

Willoughby's Beach forms a pretty solid foundation for what's to come: The lo-fi production style, the catchy songwriting, a sort of “simple complexity” to the instrumental lines... Everything that KGLW would become shines through in glimmers, but it's all a lot more unrefined. If you're looking for some lo-fi garage rock that you can throw on while you're playing a video game or having some friends over, Willoughby's has a very fun and approachable sound and vibe.

If I had to give this album a numbered rank, I would give it a THREE OUT OF FIVE. Although the album's sound is pretty average, it's clear that the minds behind it are above average and just dipping their toes into the pool of writing a proper album together. I'm excited to listen to KGLW's next release after this!
